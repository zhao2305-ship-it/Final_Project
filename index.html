Final Project – Real-Time Water Surface & Caustics

A real-time OpenGL scene focusing on animated water, lighting, and caustic effects.

Project Description

I am working on this final project by myself. The original goal of this project was to create a small real-time scene with a moving water surface. The water rises and falls over time, shows bright highlights, and changes its appearance depending on the viewing angle, similar to a real lake.

When viewed from the side, the water appears more reflective, and when viewed from above, it becomes more transparent. Under the water surface, I placed a simple ground plane where I aimed to show the soft, moving light patterns that appear when sunlight passes through water.

The entire project is built using OpenGL and GLSL shaders. Since the focus is on water motion, reflections, transparency, and caustics, the final visual result is intentionally very different from any previous assignments in the course.

Progress Update and Development Direction

At the midpoint of the project, the core structure was already in place. I had a working rendering framework and a subdivided plane that acted as the water surface. This plane was animated using time-based wave functions, allowing the surface to move smoothly over time.

I added basic lighting so the water surface could show moving highlights as the waves shift. A simple Fresnel effect was also implemented, making the water more reflective at shallow angles and clearer when viewed from above.

At that stage, the underwater ground plane was visible, and I began working on animated caustics. While the caustics were functional, they initially appeared rough and required further refinement.

As the project developed further, I expanded the original visual demo into a more interactive scene. To better demonstrate how the water reacts to dynamic objects, I added a small pinball-style interaction. Moving objects collide with boundaries and obstacles, and each collision produces strong ripples on the water surface.

Challenges and Concerns

One major concern during development was surface normal calculation. If the normals are not computed smoothly, reflections and highlights can flicker or look unstable.

Another challenge was tuning the caustic patterns. Caustics can easily become too bright or move too quickly, which breaks the illusion of realistic water. Finding the right balance took several iterations.

Performance was also an important consideration. A dense water mesh combined with multiple wave components can reduce frame rate, so I needed to balance visual quality with real-time performance.

Expected Result

The intended final result is a scene that feels like shallow, clear water. The upper part of the scene shows a moving water surface with waves and shifting reflections. Beneath the surface, the ground remains visible, and soft caustic light patterns move slowly across it.

Overall, the scene is meant to feel calm and believable, allowing the viewer to clearly observe how light, motion, and geometry interact in a water environment.
